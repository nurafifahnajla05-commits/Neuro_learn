Specific Learning Disorders

📖 Dyslexia

A specific learning disorder affecting reading and language processing

🧬

Brain Mechanisms

Dyslexia involves differences in three key brain areas in the left hemisphere:

  • Broca's area (frontal lobe) — speech production
  • Wernicke's area (temporal lobe) — language comprehension
  • Angular gyrus (parietal-temporal) — connects letters with sounds

Brain scans show reduced activation in these regions when people with dyslexia read.

🧠

Cognitive Impact

Dyslexia primarily affects:

  • Phonological processing — difficulty linking sounds to letters
  • Working memory — trouble holding verbal info
  • Reading fluency — slow, effortful reading
  • Spelling — frequent errors despite practice

Importantly, intelligence is not affected — many dyslexic individuals excel in creative thinking.

🌟

Behavioural Consequences

Day-to-day impact may include:

  • Avoiding reading aloud in class
  • Low self-esteem and academic anxiety
  • Slower task completion
  • Strengths in problem-solving, art, and big-picture thinking

With proper support (multisensory teaching, extra time, assistive tech), people with dyslexia can thrive academically and professionally.

✍️ Dysgraphia

A specific learning disorder affecting writing ability and fine motor coordination

🧬

Brain Mechanisms

Dysgraphia involves disruptions in brain areas responsible for writing:

  • Parietal lobe — visual-spatial processing & letter formation
  • Frontal lobe (premotor cortex) — motor planning for hand movements
  • Cerebellum — fine motor coordination & smooth writing
  • Working memory networks — organising thoughts into sentences

Weak connections between visual-motor integration areas make writing physically and cognitively demanding.

🧠

Cognitive Impact

Dysgraphia commonly affects:

  • Handwriting — illegible, inconsistent letter sizes
  • Spelling — difficulty even with simple words
  • Written expression — trouble organising ideas on paper
  • Fine motor skills — awkward pencil grip, hand fatigue
  • Spatial planning — uneven spacing, poor margins

Thoughts may flow well verbally but get "stuck" when writing.

🌟

Behavioural Consequences

Daily impact may include:

  • Avoiding writing tasks and homework
  • Frustration, hand cramps during writing
  • Slow note-taking in class
  • Anxiety about being judged for messy work
  • Often paired with strong verbal & creative skills

Helpful supports include typing instead of writing, voice-to-text apps, occupational therapy, and extra time on written tasks.

🔢 Dyscalculia

A specific learning disorder affecting number sense and mathematical thinking

🧬

Brain Mechanisms

Dyscalculia involves differences in brain regions that process numbers:

  • Intraparietal sulcus (IPS) — the brain's "number sense" area in the parietal lobe
  • Prefrontal cortex — working memory for calculations
  • Angular gyrus — retrieving math facts from memory

fMRI studies show reduced activation in the IPS during number tasks, making basic numerical concepts feel abstract.

🧠

Cognitive Impact

Dyscalculia commonly affects:

  • Number sense — difficulty estimating quantities
  • Math facts — trouble memorising times tables
  • Calculation — slow or error-prone arithmetic
  • Time & money — confusion with clocks, change, schedules
  • Spatial reasoning — geometry and measurement struggles

Like dyslexia, dyscalculia is not related to intelligence — affected individuals can excel in non-numerical subjects.

🌟

Behavioural Consequences

Daily impact may include:

  • Math anxiety and avoidance of numerical tasks
  • Difficulty with daily activities — budgeting, telling time
  • Low confidence in STEM subjects
  • Stress over standardised tests
  • Strengths often in language, art, and abstract reasoning

Effective supports include visual aids, manipulatives (blocks, counters), calculators, step-by-step instruction, and patient teaching approaches.

The Müller-Lyer Illusion

Look at the two horizontal lines below. Which one is longer?

🤯 Surprise! Both lines are exactly the same length!

How it works: The Müller-Lyer illusion tricks your brain because of how it interprets depth and perspective. The outward-pointing arrows (Line A) make the line look like it's coming closer to you, so your brain perceives it as shorter. The inward arrows (Line B) make it look further away, so your brain assumes it must be longer to appear that size.

Brain region involved: The occipital lobe (visual cortex) processes the raw image, but the parietal lobe interprets depth and spatial relationships — sometimes making mistakes based on past experiences with 3D shapes like room corners!
